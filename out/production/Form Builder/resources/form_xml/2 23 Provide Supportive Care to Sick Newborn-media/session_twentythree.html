Premature, low birth weight, sick newborns sometime need supportive care for a short period of time till they are stable enough to survive the new environment; and these includes thermal care, fluid management, oxygen therapy, etc.

Thermal care

In case of hypothermia

  • Keeping a neonate baby in skin-to-skin contact with the mother is an effective way of keeping the baby warm
  • Keep the baby dry and well wrapped
  • A cap can reduce heat loss. Keep the room warm (at least at 25°C).
  • Check regularly that the baby’s temperature is maintained in the range of 36.0–37.0°C (96.8–98.6°F) in axilla (Use a low-reading thermometer to ensure detection of hypothermia)
  • Pay special attention to avoid chilling the baby during an examination or investigation

Incase of hyperthermia or fever

  • Do not use antipyretic agents such as paracetamol to control fever in young babies
  • Control the environment
  • If necessary, undress the baby
  • Treat according to the couse of hyperthermia

Fluid management

Encourage the mother to breastfeed frequently to prevent hypoglycaemia. In cases where the baby is just unable to feed, give expressed breast milk by nasogastric tube. If the baby cannot feed orally, or by cup/spoon or NG tube provide IV fluid.

Indications for managing a baby with IV fluid includes

  • Unconscious or having frequent convulsions
  • Bowel obstruction, necrotizing enterocolitis, or the feeds are not tolerated,
  • Babies who are lethargic
  • Treat according to the couse of hyperthermia

If IV fluids are given, reduce the rate as the volume of oral or gastric milk feeds increases. IV fluids should ideally be given with an in-line burette to ensure the exact doses of fluids prescribed.

Increase the amount of fluid given over the first 3–5 days (total amount, oral plus IV).

Day 160 ml/kg per day
Day 280 ml/kg per day
Day 3100 ml/kg per day
Then increase to 150 ml/kg per day

When the baby tolerates oral feeds well, the amount of fluid might be increased to 180 ml/kg per day after some days. Be careful in giving parenteral IV fluids, which can quickly overhydrate a child. Do not exceed 100 ml/kg per day of IV fluids, unless the baby is dehydrated or under phototherapy or a radiant heater.

This amount is the total fluid intake a baby needs, and oral intake must be taken into account when calculating IV rates.

  • During the first 2 days of life give 10% glucose infusion IV
  • Do not use IV glucose without sodium after the first 2 days of life
  • Suitable alternative IV fluids after the first 2 days are half normal saline and 5% dextrose.

Monitor the IV infusion very carefully.

  • Use a monitoring sheet
  • Calculate the drip rate
  • Check drip rate and volume infused every hour
  • Weight the baby daily
  • Watch for facial swelling: if this occurs, reduce the IV fluid to a minimum or take out the IV line

Introduce breastfeeding or milk feeding by orogastric or nasogastric tube as soon as it is safe to do so.

Oxygen therapy

Give oxygen to neonates or young babies with any of the following:

  • Central cyanosis or gasping
  • Grunting with every breath
  • Severe lower chest wall in-drawing
  • Difficulty in feeding due to respiratory distress
  • Head nodding (i.e. a nodding movement of the head, synchronous with the respiration and indicating severe respiratory distress)

Use a pulse oximeter to guide oxygen therapy. Oxygen should be given if the oxygen saturation is ≤ 90%, and the oxygen flow should be regulated to maintain saturation of > 90%. Oxygen can be discontinued once the baby can maintain saturation > 90% in room air.

Nasal prongs are the preferred method for delivering oxygen to this age group, with a flow rate of 0.5–1 litre/min, increased to 2 litres/min in severe respiratory distress to achieve oxygen saturation >90%. Thick secretions should be cleared from the throat by intermittent suction under direct observation, if they are obstructing the airway and the baby is too weak to clear them.
