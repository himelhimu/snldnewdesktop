Neonates and young infants often present with convulsion or fits.

The most common causes of neonatal convulsions are:

  • hypoxic ischaemic encephalopathy (as a result of perinatal asphyxia)
  • central nervous system infection
  • hypoglycaemia
  • hypocalcaemia
  • Kernicterus
  • Congenital malformation
  • Idopathic

Clinical Presentation:

  • Lipsmacking, Cycling movement, flickering of hands, feet or figures, deviated and eye movement and even apoenic spells

Investigation:

  • Blood count, blood culture
  • Serum chemistries: estimations of serum glucose, calcium, magnesium, sodium
  • CSF examination\Ultrasonography of the head
  • CT scan/MRI

Management

  • Admit immediately
  • Maintain the airway and breathing. Give bag-mask ventilation with oxygen (or room air if oxygen is not available) if there is apnoea, gasping or respiratory rate too slow (< 20)
  • Give oxygen by nasal prongs if the young infant is cyanosed or in severe respiratory distress or hypoxaemic (oxygen saturation â‰¤ 90%)
  • If drowsy, unconscious or convulsing, check blood glucose. If hypo-glycaemic, give glucose IV or naso-gastrically (2 ml/kg of 10% glucose). If blood glucose cannot be measured, give empirical treatment with glucose. If you cannot insert an IV drip, give expressed breast milk or glucose through a nasogastric tube
  • Open IV channel and insert venous cannula & ensure continuous circulatory access
  • Treat convulsion with phenobarbital (loading dose 20 mg/kg IV). If convulsions persist, give further doses of phenobarbital 10 mg/kg up to a maximum of 40 mg/kg
  • If hypo-calcaemic, symptoms may settle if the infant is given 2 ml/kg of 10% calcium gluconate as a slow IV infusion, and continued with oral supplementation
  • Watch for apnoea. Always have a bag-mask available. If needed, continue phenobarbital at a maintenance dose of 5 mg/kg per day
  • Give vitamin K (if not given before)
  • Rule out central nervous system (CNS) infection. Treat accordingly
  • Monitor the infant frequently
