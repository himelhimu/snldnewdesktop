Antenatal corticosteroid (ACS) is the single most beneficial intervention for improvement of newborn outcomes among babies born between 24-34 (34+6) weeks gestational age. Steroids increase the natural production of surfactant. Surfactant is lacking in preterm babies and necessary for lung expansion during breathing. Steroids also have a protective effect on cerebral blood vessels and the intestines.

A complete course leads to:

  • 34% reduction in respiratory distress syndrome
  • 46% reduction in intra-ventricular hemorrhage
  • 54% reduction in necrotizing entero-colitis
  • 31% reduction in death

Time: ACS should be initiated to every pregnant woman presenting at 24 – 34 weeks with the likelihood of preterm delivery in next seven days.

ACS (Dexamethasone) should be given if women has one of these following conditions

  • Preterm labor (painful contractions)
  • Severe pre- eclampsia/ eclampsia
  • Preterm prematur rupture of membranes, (pPROM- sudden gush of fluid from vagina)
  • Antepartum hemorrhage (vaginal bleeding)

Other conditions, include fetal distress, multiple pregnancies, Rh-isoimmunization etc. ACS can be administered in a facility where following three conditions can be met:

  • Ability to accurately assess gestational age and determine risk of imminent preterm birth
  • Adequate care available for preterm newborns (e.g. resuscitation, Kangaroo Mother Care, adequate feeding support, treatment of infection, etc)
  • Reliable, timely and appropriate identification and treatment of maternal infection

Dose: A complete course of ACS is 4 doses of 6 mg Dexamethasone IM, each separated by 12 hours.
Or

2 doses of 12 mg Dexamethasone IM, each separated by 24 hours.

A “Quick Check” should be done for every pregnant woman immediately on arrival (within 15 min) to identify life-threatening conditions and/or labor. The quick check will also identify the main conditions leading to preterm birth. It is important to initiate ACS quickly because

  • 48 hours is required to administer the full course and receive maximum benefit from the steroids
  • There is increasing benefit with each dose and hour passed even if the full course cannot be administered prior to delivery

Quick Check

Ask, Check, Record:

  • Why did you come?
  • When do you think you should be having your baby? Fetal movement?
  • Bleeding, leaking, pain or fever?

Look, Listen, Feel:

Classify emergency for the woman if she is or has:

  • Unconscious
  • Bleeding vaginally
  • Convulsing
  • Severe abdominal pain or looks very ill
  • Headache, visual disturbances or a diastolic blood pressure > 110 or Systolic blood pressure > 160 mmHg
  • Severe difficulty breathing
  • Severe vomiting, epigastric pain
  • Fever

Continue with rapid assessment, including eligibility for ACS, and management.

If the woman appears to be in labor or complains of labour pain:

  • Assess for gestational age to determine if preterm
  • Continue with standard labour assessment

Preterm premature rupture of membranes (pPROM)

When the amniotic sac breaks prior to 34 weeks gestation in the absence of labor.

Diagnosis:

  • The typical odor of amniotic fluid confirms the diagnosis
  • Using a high-level disinfected speculum (preferably a cusco’s speculum), look for pooling of fluid in the posterior fornix; ask the woman to cough and watch for gush of fluid coming out from cervix
  • If available, swab nitrazine paper (ph paper) against the fluid. Blue (alkaline) is suggestive of amniotic fluid although blood and some infections can cause false positives
  • Report of a sudden gush of fluid is highly suggestive for rupture of membranes

Key points:

  • DO NOT perform a digital vaginal exam when leakage of fluid is suspected. This increases the risk of infection
  • Monitor for signs of infection & give antibiotics per protocol
  • Consider transfer to the most appropriate level of care for the newborn if preterm

Severe Pre-Eclampsia/ Eclampsia

Hypertension that develops or worsens during pregnancy with evidence of organ failure

Diagnosis: A diagnosis can be made if any of the following are found in the presence of BP >140/90 mm Hg

  • Systolic BP >160 mm Hg OR Diastolic BP>110 mm Hg
  • Any proteinuria
  • Impaired liver function or severe persistent right upper quadrant or epigastric pain
  • Pulmonary edema
  • New onset severe headaches or visual disturbances
  • Thrombocytopenia (platelet count less than 100,000/microliter)
  • New onset renal insufficiency

Eclampsia: when convulsion is associated with pre-eclampsia

Key points:

  • A diastolic BP of > 110 mm Hg and proteinuria is sufficient to diagnose severe pre-eclampsia.
  • Per WHO 2011 guidelines:

In women with severe pre-eclampsia, a viable fetus, and before 34 weeks of gestation, a policy of expectant management is recommended, provided that uncontrolled maternal hypertension, increasing maternal organ dysfunction or fetal distress are absent and can be monitored. Expectant management includes ACS, magnesium sulfate, antihypertensive drugs, and close maternal and fetal monitoring.

Ante-Partum Hemorrhage

Vaginal bleeding in late pregnancy (uterus above umbilicus) prior to the birth of the baby

Diagnosis:

  • Any vaginal bleeding, by maternal report and/or clinical assessment
  • Can be confirmed by ultra-sonogram
  • Additional Signs and Symptoms:
  • Intermittent or constant abdominal pain (placental abruption or ruptured uterus)
  • Fetal presentation not in pelvis; bleeding may be precipitated by intercourse (placenta previa)

Key points:

  • lDO NOT do a digital vaginal exam in the presence of vaginal bleeding
  • Bleeding could be due to placenta previa, abruptio placentae, or ruptured uterus
  • Antepartum hemorrhage can also be caused by violence or abuse
  • Up to one fifth of very preterm babies are born to mothers with antepartum hemorrhage
  • Refer to a higher level facility immediately if your facility is not equipped to manage shock, operative delivery, or a preterm baby

Preterm Labor

Regular contractions of the uterus resulting in cervical changes before 34 weeks pregnancy

Diagnosis:

  • A change in cervical dilation or effacement detected by serial vaginal examinations OR
  • 4 or more uterine contractions in 20 minutes plus 1 of the following:
    • pPROM
    • Cervical dilation >2cm
    • Effacement >50%

Additional Signs and Symptoms:

  • Cramping; pelvic pressure; backache or low back pain; excessive vaginal discharge

Key points:

  • Follow local protocol to initiate tocolytics to delay labor 48 hours in order to gain full benefit from ACS
  • No benefit, if ACS is repeated
